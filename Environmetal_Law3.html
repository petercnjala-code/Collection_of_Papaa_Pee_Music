Environmental Law Course

Ten-Part Overview of Principles, Treaties, and Practice

Page 1: Introduction

Environmental law governs human interaction with nature. It balances development with sustainability, ensuring ecosystems are preserved for future generations. It is both national and international in scope.

Page 2: Historical Evolution

The field grew after the Stockholm Conference (1972) and the Rio Earth Summit (1992). These milestones established global awareness and frameworks for environmental governance.

Page 3: Core Principles

  • No-Harm Principle
  • Precautionary Principle
  • Polluter Pays Principle
  • Sustainable Development
  • Intergenerational Equity

Page 4: International Treaties

Key agreements include the Montreal Protocol (ozone), Kyoto Protocol and Paris Agreement (climate), CITES (species trade), and the Basel Convention (hazardous waste).

Page 5: Institutions

The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), World Bank, and regional organizations enforce and support environmental law. Courts like the International Court of Justice also adjudicate disputes.

Page 6: Human Rights Linkages

Environmental law intersects with the right to health, clean water, and a safe environment. Philippe Sands emphasizes that environmental protection is now a universal human concern.

Page 7: Climate Change Law

Climate law addresses greenhouse gas emissions, adaptation, and mitigation. The Paris Agreement (2015) set global targets to limit warming to well below 2°C, with differentiated responsibilities among nations.

Page 8: Biodiversity & Conservation

The Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and CITES regulate species protection and genetic resources. National parks and protected areas are legal tools for conservation.

Page 9: Local Context (Malawi)

Malawi’s Environmental Management Act emphasizes sustainable resource use, pollution control, and community participation. It reflects global principles while addressing local challenges like deforestation and water scarcity.

Page 10: Future Directions

Environmental law is evolving to address new challenges: climate refugees, geoengineering, and digital monitoring of ecosystems. The future lies in stronger enforcement, global cooperation, and community empowerment.
